The Quality Scale: What Does 1–10 Mean?
Synthesized from conversations with Product (Yoav Yahav, Ron Tahan, Dan Dragucki, Yuval Ben Nissan) and Dev (Shahar Cohen, Tal Kalderon, Daniel Hutzler) on March 16, 2026.
The North Star

Yoav Yahav: "The first thing is to make sure your highlights are good. Everything else is secondary."
Ron Tahan: "When I say a sport is good, it's composed of several things" — highlights quality is the first thing he checks.
Shahar Cohen: "Even at our level this is very subjective — there's no single definition." But converged on bugs, manual work, client type, and content quality.

1. Content Quality (30%)

Ron #1 · Yoav #1 · Shahar #4

How good are the auto-generated highlights? Are critical events missing? Is the event order correct?

Data: Snowflake MISSING_CRITICAL_ALL_SPORTS_VU_LIST (Kesem Cohen's pipeline)

10Perfect — <0.2% missing events. Highlights feel complete and polished. 9Excellent — 0.2–0.5%. Rare misses, only edge cases. 8Strong — 0.5–1%. Occasional miss, quickly caught by ops. 7Good — 1–2%. Manageable issues. Client rarely notices. 6OK — 2–4%. Noticeable gaps. Ops fixes most before client sees. 5Mediocre — 4–8%. Regular misses. Some reach the client. 3Weak — 8–15%. Frequent issues. Client likely complains. 2Poor — 15–30%. Major gaps. Content often broken. 1Broken — 30%+. More broken than working.

2. Data Provider (20%)

Ron #2 · Yoav · Shahar

How reliable is the data feed? Premium providers (Opta, Sportradar) = rich, fast, accurate. Lower tiers = gaps, delays, manual work.

Data: Snowflake MRR_INGESTIONMANAGERTODATAPROVIDER + industry reputation

10Tier 1 — Opta, Sportradar, ProTennis, OBS. Gold standard. Multiple T1 available. 7Tier 2 — Statscore, NRL, Genius/FIBA, league-official data. Solid but narrower. 4Tier 3 — DSG, Enetpulse, Kayo, regional providers. Adequate, limited params. 2Tier 4 — Manual only, no automated data. Yoav: "Only with good data like the Olympics."

3. Bug Density (15%)

Ron #3 · Shahar #1, #2

How many bugs affect this sport? Shahar: "Manual work eventually causes bugs." Normalized per 1000 VUs, weighted by priority.

Data: Jira sport keyword search (YTD 2026), priority-weighted

10Zero bugs. Clean, stable pipeline. 8Rare bugs, low severity. Quickly resolved. 5Regular bugs, some P1s. Dev attention needed. 3Frequent bugs or P0s. Stability concern. 1Constant fires. Sport is a burden to maintain.

4. Scale & Maturity (10%)

Volume signal

How many VUs does this sport produce? Higher volume = more battle-tested, more client value, more data to learn from.

Data: Snowflake VU counts YTD 2026

1010K+ VUs. Massive scale, well proven. 85K–10K VUs. Strong volume. 61K–5K VUs. Meaningful but moderate. 3100–500 VUs. Limited testing. 1<100 VUs. Barely active.

5. Storylines (15%)

Ron #5 · Yoav · Shahar #4

How many distinct AVGen output video types are actively used? Each type represents a different storyline: Game Highlights, Condensed Game, Player Highlights, Top X Plays, Period Highlights, Team Highlights, etc.

Data: MSSQL avgen.Rules × dbo.Systems — distinct OutputVideoType per ResolverType (active rules only)

1018–20 types — Full suite: post-game, condensed, player, top X, period, team, compiled, filter, etc. 916–17 types — Near-complete, missing only niche storylines. 814–15 types — Strong variety. Covers all major use cases. 712–13 types — Good breadth. Some advanced types missing. 610–11 types — Decent coverage, gaps in compilation/period content. 58–9 types — Basic set + a few extras. 46–7 types — Limited to essentials. 34–5 types — Minimal. 22–3 types — Bare minimum (highlight + filter). 10–1 type — Practically no AVGen storylines.

6. Vertical / Stories (10%)

Ron #5 · Shahar #4

Does this sport have SDK Story Pages? Story Pages deliver an interactive vertical (9:16) experience — scores, events, video clips — similar to Instagram Stories but sport-specific. Measures how close vertical is to the horizontal video capability.

Data: MSSQL Stories.StoryConfiguration × dbo.Systems — distinct configs per ResolverType

1050+ story configs — massive adoption across many clients/leagues. 920–49 configs — strong adoption, multiple major clients using stories. 810–19 configs — good adoption, several clients with story pages. 75–9 configs — moderate adoption, a handful of clients. 53–4 configs — early adoption, limited client set. 31–2 configs — just starting, proof of concept. 10 configs — no story pages. Vertical gap vs horizontal.
Qualitative signals not yet scored (future)

Client Impact — # of clients, client tier, AM satisfaction (Shahar: "PGA is few clients but huge"). Requires Salesforce integration.
Tagging Quality — E2E accuracy, ops correction rate (Ron #4). Kesem is building this in Snowflake.
Investment / Maintenance Burden — Dev effort, ops hours, manual work (Shahar: "manual work causes bugs"). Requires sprint tracking.
Dev Engagement — Shahar: "the interest/lack of interest of developers in the sport, and the level of investment required."

Data Provider Tiers
Source: Snowflake MRR.MRR_INGESTIONMANAGERTODATAPROVIDER. Tier assignment based on provider reputation, data richness, and reliability.
Tier Definitions

Tier 1 (score 10): Opta, Sportradar (SR_*), ProTennis, OBS, NBAStatsFeeds, NHL, MLB, NFL, PGA — Industry leaders, official league feeds, rich parameters, fast delivery.
Tier 2 (score 7): Statscore, Genius/FIBA, Euroleague, NRL, EHF, CEV, AFL, HockeyTech, IIHF — Solid specialized or league-specific providers.
Tier 3 (score 4): DSG, Enetpulse, Kayo, FoxAustralia, Ellipse, SportzInteractive, AthStat, Kadamba, regional providers — Adequate coverage, limited parameters.
Tier 4 (score 2): Manual tagging only, no automated data feed.
